Watch List

UX/UI Design | Idelic

    Problem

    Industry Background and User Problems

    The trucking industry has a handful of problems. One is it's astonishingly high driver turnover due to drivers jumping from fleet to fleet for very slight pay bumps and not feeling they are treated well. Two, many trucking fleets still use excel spreadsheets to track all of their data. This can easily get out of hand when a fleet has just a few hundred drivers because there are several silioed data systems, federal required documents, and recording of events like inspections, violations, collisions, etc. Lastly, events like collisions with other vehicles can easily get up to tens of thousands of dollars in fees and a mound of paper work for the fleet's safety team.

    Idelic's Watch List uses Machine Learning to analyze a truck driver's risk level on the road by looking at an array of factors including (but not limited to) previous collisions, road violations, hours of service violations, and telematics alerts (speeding, harsh braking, harsh cornering).

    Design Challenges

    Some challenges included:

    1. Balance between user needs and blowing up development effort.
    2. Balance between volume of data and cognitive overload.

    Task

    The task was to create a new more robust, yet simple workflow that enabled a fleet's safety team to:

    1. Identify the fleet's riskest drivers.
    2. Briefly analyze that driver's history.
    3. Assign a performance improvement plan. Performance improvement plans (previous UX project with Idelic) are prestrictive plans with multi-step, multi-touch engages between the driver and their manager to work the driver's their root issue.

    Research

    Interviews

    Although requirements around user permissions can vary by fleet, we believed mostly upper management (VP of the Safety team) would be using this feature based on previous findings from other features, persona behaviors, and persona responsibiliies. At the same time we were exploring the idea of putting a condensed view of the Watch List on a Executive Dashboard (See KPI Dashboard for more details on this project). Because of this we conducted phone interviews with them.

    4 different interviews were conducted with upper management of varying types of trucking fleets. Findings confirmed that a condensed version of the Watch List should be on the user's dashboard due to their limited time. Also, finding pointed to some of the key factors managers look for when assessing a driver's behavior. Some include collisions and telematic events.

    Iteration 1

    Designing Reasoning

    Because some fleets have so many drivers, they'll need to go through them very efficiently. Thus the first iteration enabled the user to complete the entire workflow (viewing riskest drivers, analyzing behavior, and taking action) from the dashboard. This was done by clicking on a driver, which opens a modal like side panel from Microsoft's Office Fabric UI library. Or if desired, they could go a separate deticated screen to view the Watch List full window width.

    So first, the user would look at the list of drivers and select a driver based on the safety score given by the machine learning model. Next, they would view the driver's history and determine what their root issues. Last, the user would go through a step by step wizard to sign a performance improvement plan.

    Findings From Testing

    5 users were tested from varying companies. Findings showed that the ability to assign prescriptive plans in a highly formated manner via a step by step wizard UI were highly desired. However, the modal panel pattern was constraining in terms of space and would overlap the Watch List itself when triggered. Additionally, it was found being explicit in labeling just very neccassary as many users questioned certain parts of graphs and other smaller UI elements. Lastly, it was noted that users might want to be to esculate the employee's disciplinary status and assign a letter, which is common in trucking fleets. Letters inform the driver of what they did wrong and require written confirmation from the driver that they've recieved it.

    Overall, findings indicated we were on the correct path. Several participants enthusiastically replied "Yes!" when asked if they would be like to stay in the loop on the features progress and providing additional insight.

    Iteration 2

    Design Reasoning

    Due to the constrains of the modal panel, it was decided to still have the Watch List on the Dashboard (user task 1); however, analyzing (user task 2) and prescribing a plan (user task 3) were moved.

    Inspired by some other B2B UIs, a new idea came about to click on a driver and the table shrinks to a list. This gave enough room to display the driver's information and transition to a wizard UI on the right side, yet still enabling the user to see where the driver was in relation to others on the Watch List. Now, if users clicked on the header they would see a full width data table of the Watch List on a new screen and then clicking a driver would trigger the table become a list and show the driver info. If users clicks on a driver, then it directs them to the new page, but would already trigger the animation to show the Watch List as a condensed list and open the driver's profile. Plus, an additional step was added to the wizard, to allow the user to esculate the driver's disciplinary status. Lastly, additional labeling was added.

    Findings From Testing

    Some users were involved in testing and there were no questions evolving around labeling. There was a concern that the new idea on animation and splitting the screen would cause some cognitive overload, leading to confusion; however, users followed along just fine. Additionally, there was a concern users would have difficulties with how telematic alerts (speeding, harsh braking, harsh cornering) are displayed since they can occur VERY frequently. However, the new condensed viewing of telematics enabled the user to dive deeper if needed.

    Training

    UX/UI Design | Idelic

      Problem

      Industry Background

      First, CDL drivers have a lot of federal regulations they have to abide by, which requires certain certifications and training. This also requires the company’s safety team to keep track if those drivers are up-to-date. If they don’t and the driver gets into pulled over, this can result in higher fines; the vehicle becoming out of service (OoS) which prevents completion of a delivery; or even a negative impact on the company’s CSA score. Although the Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration (FMCSA) is looking to move from the CSA score to an IRT model, this can potentially still hurt a company’s score.

      User Problems

      Previous iterations of the training module only let a user certify/pass a driver one by one, which would take an hour or more for a trainer or manager to go through training classes that had 50 or more drivers. On top of this, fleets have different ways of approaching assigning and certifying a driver passed depending on their size and financial resources.

      Design Limitations and Challenges

      Some limitations and challenges included:

      1. First project implementing a new design system (Microsoft’s Fabric UI with Typescript).
      2. An urgency to begin development from customers.
      3. Limited time and team members.
      4. Other projects in progress as well.

      Task

      The task was to create a simple way to assign and track training classes and attendees for different company’s needs like:

      1. Integrating with other training management software that fead data to and from.
      2. Dealing with in-person vs online video trainings.

      Research

      Observations and Interviews

      Due to limited time, 2 observations and 3 interviews were conducted with only two different companies; however, their needs differed. One didn’t not have another system to for the safety team for drivers to do video training. They did all training in-person. The second company did very little in-person training and primarily relied on drivers signing into an integrated system to do online video trainings, but they wanted our system to allow the managers to assign and track driver’s trainings that are nearing expiration.

      Observations involved watching users walk through each step of what they would do on other training management systems and encouraging users mention the software’s pros and cons. Interviews involved tell us about other software’s pros and cons and understanding their differing business processes.

      Iteration 1

      Designing Reasoning

      Based on conversations with customers, this would need to allow for interesting edge cases and plenty of forgiveness for editing, yet be able to guide users through each requirement. Because users would do the same or very similar trainings in the admin panel, a place to configure settings of the software. It contains things like the training name, training description, driving positions that require different trainings, and expiration for the training. All of which users, would rarely edit.

      Next is the creation of classes based on a training course template. Classes are an instance of a training course that contains a participant roster, date, and time. Due to it’s multi-step process, a wizard UI pattern was thought to be the best approach. Because the previous training module just spit out a list of drivers with the training type, it seemed to cause cognitive overload for users. To solve this, we added a step at the beginning. Users would first select the training type that showed the amount of drivers past due, due within 30 days, and due within 90 days for that training type. This acted as a filter and allowed the user to easily focus on one training type. Step 2 users would select who would attend that class and be able to filter by properties like terminal, date, and position. Step 3 users set the trainer, date, time, and location for the training. Last, users reviewed all of the information and confirmed the creation of the class.

      Lastly, users had to be able to mark whether or not participants attended and passed the class. This was in a separate section of the training module where they could a running history of classes with filters to find classes. The list needed visual indicators to show which classes are pending review and already reviewed classes. Once users clicked on a class, they had to exempt a driver from the roster, fail one, and pass the rest.

      Findings From Testing

      5 participants from 3 different companies tested a paper prototype and feedback was very positive. In addition, stock holders from upper management were consulted with the findings. All 5 participants went through the class creation process with ease. 2 participants questioned how to switch from class creation and the running history of classes. However, after a short period were able to see how to which views. When participants were asked to find the Forklift class they created, they did it with ease. When the panel slid out from the right side, 3 participants would there was a lack of visual indicator to understand the top part of the modal panel contained the class details and the second half had the class roster. Once they distinguished this, 5 participants understood how to mark off the attendees, some minor edits were required to help understand what to do.

      Iteration 2

      Design Reasoning

      Because of time restrictions and the positive feedback for participants, development began. The wizard UI pattern for class creation did not require major edits. However, it was required to helping users understand what to do with the roster of drivers, and indicating that the fields were for the class details. It was decided that we would keep the modal panel to show the roster and class details, and to not expand the already large scope of development effort. To distinguish the two parts, we put the two parts into tabs: the first was for the roster and the second for the class details. Plus, participants and stack holders mentioned the need to document any details of the class. For example, trainers might need to state that certain drivers were more engaged than others. This was added to the second tab.
